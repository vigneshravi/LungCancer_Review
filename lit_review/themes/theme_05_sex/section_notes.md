## What the literature establishes

Sex and gender differences in lung cancer are well-documented but mechanistically underexplored. Women, particularly never-smokers, show higher rates of EGFR mutations in LUAD. Estrogen receptor signaling has been implicated in lung cancer biology, with preclinical evidence suggesting estrogen promotes proliferation through non-genomic signaling pathways.

Immunotherapy outcomes show sex-specific patterns, with meta-analyses suggesting differential benefit from immune checkpoint inhibitors between sexes, though findings are not entirely consistent. Loss of Y chromosome in male tumors has been associated with altered immune surveillance and immunotherapy response. Epidemiological data consistently show women developing lung cancer at younger ages and with lighter smoking histories.

## Where consensus exists

- Women have higher EGFR mutation rates in LUAD, especially never-smokers
- Sex-specific differences in immunotherapy outcomes exist but effect sizes are debated
- Estrogen receptor biology is relevant to lung cancer pathogenesis
- Sex-stratified analyses remain underperformed in most clinical and genomic studies

## Where the field disagrees or is uncertain

- Whether sex-based differences in immunotherapy benefit are biologically driven or confounded
- The role of estrogen receptor signaling: direct oncogenic driver vs. modifier
- Whether sex-stratified dosing is warranted for targeted therapies
- How to separate sex (biological) from gender (sociocultural) effects in outcomes research

## Gaps and open questions

- Mechanistic studies of sex hormone interactions with lung cancer signaling are sparse
- Sex-stratified drug response data largely absent from pharmacogenomic databases
- Y chromosome loss in lung cancer poorly characterized
- Pregnancy-associated lung cancer understudied molecularly
- Sex-specific biomarker panels for immunotherapy selection absent
