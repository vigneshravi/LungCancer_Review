# Theme 10 — Synthesis

*Theme: Targeted therapy access, translational bottleneck, real-world outcomes*
*Total papers: 6431 | Tier A: 0 | Tier B: 67*

## What the literature establishes

Despite advances in precision oncology, significant translational bottlenecks persist in lung cancer. Biomarker testing rates remain suboptimal, with disparities by race, socioeconomic status, geographic location, and insurance coverage. Many molecularly defined patients never receive matched targeted therapies due to access barriers, testing gaps, or rapid clinical deterioration.

Real-world evidence studies show that outcomes for NSCLC patients receiving targeted therapies or immunotherapy are more heterogeneous than clinical trial results, reflecting broader patient populations and practice variation. Clinical trial representation of women, minorities, and older adults remains inadequate.

## Where consensus exists

- Biomarker testing rates are suboptimal and inequitable
- Real-world outcomes are more heterogeneous than clinical trial results
- Disparities in precision oncology access exist along racial, socioeconomic, and geographic lines
- Guideline-concordant care improves outcomes but adherence varies

## Where the field disagrees or is uncertain

- Whether real-world evidence should inform treatment guidelines to the same degree as RCTs
- How to address structural barriers to biomarker testing
- Whether molecular tumor boards improve outcomes vs. standard oncology practice
- The role of direct-to-consumer genomic testing in oncology

## Gaps and open questions

- Interventions to improve biomarker testing rates not well evaluated
- Real-world evidence from low- and middle-income countries is minimal
- Patient-reported outcomes rarely integrated with molecular data
- Impact of molecular tumor boards vs. standard care not rigorously compared
- Cost-effectiveness of multi-omics precision oncology not established
