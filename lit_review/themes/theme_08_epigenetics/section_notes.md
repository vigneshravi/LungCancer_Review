# Theme 08 — Synthesis

*Theme: Epigenetics in lung cancer*
*Total papers: 5902 | Tier A: 0 | Tier B: 91*

## What the literature establishes

Epigenetic alterations are pervasive in lung cancer and operate across multiple regulatory layers. DNA methylation changes, including hypermethylation of tumor suppressors (CDKN2A, RASSF1A, MGMT) and global hypomethylation, are early events in lung carcinogenesis. Methylation-based classifiers can distinguish NSCLC subtypes and predict prognosis.

Non-coding RNAs, particularly miRNAs and lncRNAs, function as both oncogenes and tumor suppressors in lung cancer. Chromatin accessibility mapping via ATAC-seq has revealed enhancer landscapes that define lung cancer subtypes and identify master transcription factors. cfDNA methylation-based liquid biopsy approaches show promise for early detection and minimal residual disease monitoring.

## Where consensus exists

- DNA methylation changes are early events in lung carcinogenesis
- Hypermethylation of specific tumor suppressors is reproducible across studies
- Non-coding RNAs play functional roles in lung cancer biology
- cfDNA methylation shows promise as a liquid biopsy biomarker

## Where the field disagrees or is uncertain

- Which epigenetic marks are drivers vs. passengers in lung carcinogenesis
- Functional significance of many reported lncRNA associations
- Reproducibility of miRNA prognostic signatures across populations
- Whether epigenetic therapies can be effective in solid tumors

## Gaps and open questions

- Causal vs. consequential epigenetic changes not well distinguished
- Single-cell epigenomic profiling in lung cancer is limited
- Epigenetic therapy clinical trials in lung cancer are few
- Integration of epigenomic with genomic/transcriptomic data for subtyping needs work
- Longitudinal epigenetic monitoring through treatment underexplored
