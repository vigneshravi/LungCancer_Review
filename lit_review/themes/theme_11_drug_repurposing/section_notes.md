# Theme 11 — Synthesis

*Theme: Drug repurposing in oncology*
*Total papers: 6386 | Tier A: 0 | Tier B: 24*

## What the literature establishes

Drug repurposing leverages computational and multi-omics approaches to identify new therapeutic indications for existing drugs. Connectivity Map (CMap) approaches have identified candidates by matching drug-induced gene expression signatures to disease signatures. Pharmacogenomic databases (GDSC, CCLE, PRISM) provide large-scale drug sensitivity data linked to molecular features.

Synthetic lethality approaches, particularly exploiting KRAS or STK11/KEAP1 dependencies, offer targeted therapeutic strategies. Network pharmacology integrates drug-target-disease networks for systematic identification of repurposing candidates. Multi-omics integration enhances drug response prediction beyond single-omics approaches.

## Where consensus exists

- Computational drug repurposing can identify viable candidates
- Pharmacogenomic databases enable systematic drug-gene interaction analysis
- Multi-omics integration enhances drug response prediction
- Synthetic lethality is a promising strategy for KRAS-mutant lung cancers

## Where the field disagrees or is uncertain

- Whether computational drug repurposing predictions translate to clinical success at meaningful rates
- Optimal experimental validation frameworks for repurposing candidates
- How to prioritize among many predicted candidates for clinical development
- Whether network pharmacology approaches capture real biological complexity

## Gaps and open questions

- Translation of computational predictions to clinical trials remains low
- Drug combination optimization using multi-omics data is nascent
- Lung cancer-specific drug repurposing studies are fewer than pan-cancer
- Dimethyl fumarate and other KEAP1-targeting repurposing candidates need clinical data
- Patient-derived organoid validation of repurposing candidates limited
