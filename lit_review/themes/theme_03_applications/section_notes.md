# Theme 03 — Synthesis

*Theme: Multi-omics applications in lung cancer*
*Total papers: 2648 | Tier A: 1 | Tier B: 127*

## What the literature establishes

Multi-omics integration has been applied to lung cancer for subtype discovery, prognosis prediction, and therapeutic stratification. Proteogenomic studies from CPTAC and collaborating groups have revealed that protein-level alterations do not always mirror transcriptomic changes, highlighting the importance of integrating multiple data layers. These studies identified novel therapeutic targets through phosphoproteomics that were invisible at the genomic level.

Integrative analyses combining transcriptomic, methylomic, and proteomic data have refined NSCLC subtyping beyond traditional histopathological classification. Multi-omics approaches to tumor microenvironment characterization have revealed immune-excluded and immune-infiltrated subtypes with differential immunotherapy response. Multi-omics-based prognostic signatures have shown incremental improvement over single-omics approaches, though external validation remains limited.

## Where consensus exists

- Proteogenomic integration reveals clinically relevant features invisible to genomics alone
- Multi-omics prognosis models modestly outperform single-omics signatures
- Tumor microenvironment characterization benefits from multi-omics approaches
- External validation of multi-omics signatures remains the major bottleneck

## Where the field disagrees or is uncertain

- Whether multi-omics signatures offer sufficient incremental value over simpler models
- Optimal omics combination for specific clinical endpoints
- How to validate multi-omics findings when independent multi-omics datasets are rare
- Whether batch effects across cohorts compromise multi-omics discoveries

## Gaps and open questions

- Very few lung cancer multi-omics studies include matched pharmacogenomic data
- Limited application of multi-omics to radiation response in lung cancer
- Early detection multi-omics approaches are in early stages
- No multi-omics studies focused specifically on NSCLC in never-smokers
- Sex-stratified multi-omics analyses nearly absent
